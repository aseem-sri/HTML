Movie Review: Tumbbad

Tumbbad (2018) is a groundbreaking masterpiece of Indian cinema that seamlessly blends folk horror, dark fantasy, and socio-political commentary. Directed by Rahi Anil Barve and Anand Gandhi, the film uses a fictionalized mythological framework to deliver a scathing critique of human greed across three distinct generations.

Movie Rating: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐☆ (9.5 out of 10)

Narrative Architecture & Chapter-Wise Review

The film structuralizes its narrative into three distinct acts spanning from 1918 to post-Independence India. This reflects the evolution of human greed alongside changing sociopolitical structures.

Chapter I: The Genesis (1918)

This segment introduces the rainy, cursed village of Tumbbad where a young Vinayak Rao and his mother serve a decaying feudal lord who possesses hidden gold. It sets a foundation of raw dread with the depiction of Vinayak’s monstrous, cursed great-grandmother. It illustrates how feudalism breeds extreme economic deprivation, shaping Vinayak’s lifelong obsession with wealth.

Chapter II: The Accumulation (1933)

A grown-up Vinayak returns to the crumbling family estate and figures out how to outsmart the fallen god Hastar to extract gold coins. This act operates like a high-stakes psychological thriller. Set during British colonial India, it symbolizes colonial exploitation where Vinayak extracts raw wealth from the earth without recognizing the moral decay eating away at his soul.

Chapter III: The Legacy & Climax (1947)

On the eve of Indian Independence, Vinayak trains his physically disabled son, Pandurang, to enter Hastar's womb. The tension escalates dramatically as father and son design a high-risk strategy to retrieve maximum gold at once. The shift to modern capitalism reveals that unchecked greed multiplies rather than satisfies human desire, threatening total destruction as the cycle passes down.

Core Themes & Allegorical Depth

  • The Myth of Hastar: The script invents folklore about the first-born of the Mother Goddess who claimed all gold but was denied food. This functions as a metaphor for the wealthy who remain perpetually starved of spiritual fulfillment.
  • The Poison of Greed: Explicitly framed by an opening Mahatma Gandhi quote, the film proves that nature provides enough for human need, but never for human greed.
  • Caste Privilege and Patriarchy: The filmmakers subtly address the corrupting nature of upper-caste lineage and entitlement where women are treated as property or collateral.

Technical & Creative Craftsmanship

  • Cinematography: Pankaj Kumar uses a natural, muted color palette. The persistent, gloomy monsoon rain reflects the internal corruption of the characters.
  • Production Design: Nitin Zihani Choudhary and Rakesh Yadav craft labyrinthine corridors and a subterranean, flesh-toned womb that look incredibly real.
  • Sound & Score: Jesper Kyd avoids standard jump-scare noises, building an eerie, operatic wall of sound that enhances the heavy atmosphere of dread.
  • VFX & Creature Design: The visual effects balance practical make-up with digital animation, keeping the creature design for Hastar terrifyingly unique.

Final Verdict & Cinematic Legacy

Tumbbad completely redefines the horror genre in Indian cinema. It replaces cheap jump scares with a slow-burning atmospheric dread and profound philosophical depth. It stands out as an international benchmark for folk-horror filmmaking.
